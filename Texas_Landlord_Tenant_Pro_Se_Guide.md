## 1. EVICTION DEFENSE (TENANT SIDE)

### 1.1 Notice Requirements Before Eviction

Before a landlord can file an eviction lawsuit, they **must** provide written notice to vacate. The landlord cannot file suit before completing this step.

**Types of Pre-Suit Notice:**

| Notice Type | When Used | Minimum Period | Authority |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Notice to Vacate** | Lease violation, holdover, or general termination | 3 days (unless lease specifies different period) | Tex. Prop. Code 24.005 |
| **Notice to Pay Rent or Vacate** | Nonpayment of rent only (if tenant paid timely the previous month) | 3 days (unless lease specifies shorter) | Tex. Prop. Code 24.005 |
| **Month-to-Month Termination** | Ending a month-to-month tenancy | One full rental period (typically 30 days) | Tex. Prop. Code 91.001 |
| **Public/Subsidized Housing** | Federally assisted housing | 30 days; must have "good cause" | Federal regulations |
| **CARES Act Covered Properties** | Properties with federal program participation or federally-backed mortgages | 30 days | Federal CARES Act |

**Acceptable Delivery Methods for Notice to Vacate:**
- Personal delivery to tenant or any person residing at the premises who is 16 years of age or older
- Placed inside the premises at a conspicuous location (e.g., inside the front door)
- Sent by first class mail, certified mail, registered mail, or delivery service with tracking
- Affixed to the outside of the front door (if other methods unavailable)
- Email or electronic portal (only if tenant agreed in writing, e.g., in lease) -- *new under SB 38*

**Critical Rule**: The 3-day notice period begins the day AFTER the notice is delivered (not the day of delivery). If delivered by mail, add time for mail delivery.

### 1.2 How to Answer an Eviction Petition (Forcible Detainer Suit)

When you are served with an eviction citation, you should:

**Step 1: Read the citation carefully.** Note the hearing date, the court (Justice of the Peace precinct), and the claims made.

**Step 2: File a Written Answer.** While not strictly required in standard cases, filing an Answer is strongly recommended because:
- It preserves your defenses on the record
- It prevents a default judgment if something prevents you from attending the hearing
- It is required for Summary Disposition cases (you have only **4 days** to respond or you lose by default)

**Where to File:** The Justice Court that issued the Eviction Citation, in the precinct where the property is located. You may file online at **efiletexas.gov** or in person with the court clerk.

**Filing Deadlines:**
- **Standard eviction**: Anytime before or on the day of the hearing
- **Summary Disposition** (squatter removal): Within **4 days** of service -- failure to respond results in judgment without a hearing
- **County Court appeal**: Within **8 days** after County Court receives your case

**What to Include in Your Answer:**
- Case number (Cause No.) exactly as shown on the citation
- Your name, last 3 digits of driver's license and SSN
- Which allegations you deny
- Your affirmative defenses (see Section 1.4)
- Request for jury trial (if desired -- must file at least 3 days before trial; costs $22 or file Statement of Inability to Pay)
- Certificate of Service (confirming you sent a copy to the plaintiff)
- Attach supporting documents (lease, payment receipts, photos, etc.)

**Step 3: Serve the Plaintiff.** On the same day you file, send the plaintiff or their attorney a copy via hand delivery, certified mail, fax, email (if consented), or e-filing system.

**Note on SB 38 (effective Jan 1, 2026):** Tenants can no longer file counterclaims in an eviction proceeding. Eviction hearings are now restricted to the single issue of possession. Any monetary claims must be filed as a separate lawsuit.

### 1.3 JP Court Eviction Process and Timeline

| Step | Timeline | Details |
|---|---|---|
| **Notice to Vacate served** | Day 0 | Landlord delivers written notice |
| **Notice period expires** | Day 3+ | Landlord may now file suit (not before) |
| **Eviction petition filed in JP Court** | After notice period | Filed in the precinct where the property is located |
| **Citation served on tenant** | At least 4 days before hearing | By sheriff, constable, or authorized server |
| **Hearing date** | 10-21 days after filing | Set by the court |
| **Tenant files Answer** | Before or at hearing | Recommended but not required (except Summary Disposition) |
| **Jury trial request deadline** | 3 days before trial | $22 fee or Statement of Inability to Pay |
| **Judgment entered** | At hearing | Court rules for landlord or tenant |
| **Appeal deadline** | 5 days after judgment | Includes weekends and holidays |
| **Writ of Possession available** | 6th day after judgment | Only if no appeal filed |
| **Constable posts 24-hour notice** | After writ issued | Warning posted on front door |
| **Tenant physically removed** | 24+ hours after posting | Constable cannot remove during rain, sleet, or snow |

### 1.4 Defenses Available to Tenants

**Procedural Defenses:**

1. **Improper Notice / No Notice ("Texas Notice" defense)**
   - Landlord failed to provide any Notice to Vacate
   - Notice did not comply with 3-day minimum (or lease-specified period)
   - Notice was not properly delivered
   - Insufficient time between notice expiration and filing suit
   - *Authority: Tex. Prop. Code 24.005*

2. **Filed Too Soon**
   - The eviction petition was filed before the notice period expired
   - Citation was served fewer than 4 days before the hearing

3. **Wrong Court / Improper Venue**
   - Case filed in the wrong precinct (must be where property is located)

4. **Improper Service**
   - Tenant was not properly served with the citation

5. **Plaintiff Lacks Standing**
   - The person filing is not the landlord, property owner, or authorized agent
   - Co-tenant trying to evict another co-tenant (not allowed in eviction court)

**Substantive Defenses:**

6. **Rent Was Paid**
   - Full payment was made but landlord filed anyway
   - Attach proof of payment (receipts, bank statements, money order stubs)

7. **Landlord Refused Payment**
   - Tenant attempted to pay on time but landlord refused or payment portal was down

8. **Right to Pay Late Rent (Right to Cure)**
   - If a tenant has paid rent timely during the entire lease term and has never received a Notice to Pay Rent or Vacate, the tenant may have one opportunity to pay late
   - *Note: This right applies only once per lease term*

9. **Waiver**
   - Landlord agreed not to evict (written agreement, acceptance of rent after violation)
   - Landlord accepted rental assistance funds covering the period in dispute
   - Landlord routinely accepted late rent, establishing a pattern/waiver

10. **Retaliation (Tex. Prop. Code 92.331-92.333)**
    - Eviction filed within **6 months** of tenant:
      - Requesting repairs in good faith
      - Reporting code violations to government authorities
      - Reporting utility issues to government agencies
      - Establishing or participating in a tenant organization
      - Exercising any right under the lease or law
    - **Remedies for proven retaliation**: One month's rent + $500 civil penalty + actual damages + court costs + attorney's fees
    - **Exception**: Retaliation defense does NOT apply if tenant is delinquent on rent at the time of the protected activity

11. **Discrimination**
    - Eviction based on race, color, national origin, religion, sex, familial status, or disability
    - *Authority: Federal Fair Housing Act; Texas Property Code Chapter 301*

12. **Habitability / Failure to Repair**
    - Landlord failed to maintain habitable conditions materially affecting health/safety
    - Must have followed repair notice procedures under Tex. Prop. Code 92.056
    - *Note: Under SB 38, this must be raised as a defense, not a counterclaim*

13. **Failure to Mitigate**
    - Landlord failed to use reasonable efforts to re-let the property
    - *Authority: Tex. Prop. Code 91.006*

14. **CARES Act / Public Housing Notice**
    - Covered property without required 30-day notice
    - Public Housing Authority without required 30-day Notice to Pay

### 1.5 Right to Cure / Right to Redeem

- **Notice to Pay Rent or Vacate**: If the tenant receives this notice (available only when tenant paid timely the previous rental period), the tenant has the option to pay the full delinquent rent within the notice period to avoid eviction
- **One-Time Late Payment Right**: If a tenant has never been late during the entire lease term and has never received a Notice to Pay, they may have a one-time right to pay late
- **After Judgment**: There is generally no statutory right to cure after an eviction judgment is entered. The tenant must appeal within 5 days or face the writ of possession.

### 1.6 Trial Process in JP Court

**Before Trial:**
- Arrive early and check in with the court clerk
- Dress professionally; address the judge as "Your Honor"
- Bring all evidence organized: lease, payment records, photos, correspondence, repair requests, witness contact information
- If you want a jury trial, you must have requested it at least 3 days before trial

**At Trial:**
- The landlord (plaintiff) presents their case first
- The landlord must prove: (1) proper notice was given, (2) proper grounds for eviction exist, (3) proper service was completed
- Tenant (defendant) may cross-examine landlord's witnesses
- Tenant then presents their defense, calling witnesses and introducing evidence
- Landlord may cross-examine tenant's witnesses
- Both sides make closing arguments
- Judge (or jury) renders judgment

**Evidence Tips for Pro Se Litigants:**
- Bring the original AND at least 3 copies of every document (for judge, opposing party, and yourself)
- Photos should be printed, not just on your phone
- Text messages and emails should be printed with dates visible
- Bring witnesses who can testify to relevant facts (e.g., repair requests, rent payments)
- Keep your presentation brief and focused on the key issues

### 1.7 Appeal to County Court

If you lose in JP Court, you have **5 days from the date the judgment is signed** to appeal. This deadline includes weekends and holidays. If the court closes before 5 PM on day 5, you may file the next business day.

**Three Ways to Perfect an Appeal:**

| Method | Description | Details |
|---|---|---|
| **Appeal Bond** | A promise to pay the judgment if you lose | Typically set at one month's rent; requires two sureties (cosigners) |
| **Cash Deposit** | Pay deposit to court registry | Usually equal to one month's rent; no sureties needed |
| **Statement of Inability to Pay** | Pauper's affidavit / affidavit of indigency | Swear to financial hardship; opposing party may challenge |

**Rent Payments During Appeal:**
- You **must** pay rent into the court registry to remain in the property during the appeal
- **First payment**: Due within 5 days of filing the appeal (paid to Justice Court)
- **Ongoing payments**: Monthly payments to County Court once the case transfers (typically 6-10 days)
- **Minimum payment**: At least $250/month, even if you normally pay no rent (unless subsidized housing)
- **Failure to pay rent into the registry can result in the appeal being dismissed**
- Under SB 38, tenants must now swear under penalty of perjury that the appeal is made in good faith and not for delay

**De Novo Trial in County Court:**
- The case is tried **completely fresh** as if the JP Court trial never happened
- You may present new evidence and new arguments
- County Court should hold the trial within **21 days** of receiving the case
- If you did not file a written answer in JP Court, you must file one within **8 days** after County Court receives your case
- County Court filing fee is due within **20 days** (if appealing by bond or deposit)
- You may request a court-appointed attorney in counties with pro bono programs

**Further Appeal to Court of Appeals:**
- If you lose in County Court, you may appeal by filing a **supersedeas bond** within **10 days** of the County Court judgment
- The supersedeas bond amount is set by the County Court considering: value of rents likely to accrue, potential damages from delay, and other appropriate amounts
- This level of appeal is significantly more complex; strongly consider retaining an attorney

### 1.8 Writ of Possession Timeline

| Event | Timeline | Authority |
|---|---|---|
| Judgment entered | Day 0 | -- |
| Appeal deadline expires | Day 5 (if no appeal filed) | Tex. Prop. Code Ch. 24 |
| Writ of Possession available | Day 6+ (if no appeal) | Tex. Prop. Code 24.0061 |
| Constable must serve writ | Within 5 days of issuance (3 business days under SB 38) | TRCP Rule 510.8 |
| 24-hour warning posted on door | After constable receives writ | Tex. Prop. Code 24.0061 |
| Physical removal | 24+ hours after warning posted | -- |
| Writ expires if not executed | 60 days after judgment (90 days for good cause) | Tex. Prop. Code 24.0061 |

**Important Rules:**
- The warning notice must be at least 8.5 x 11 inches posted on the exterior front door
- Constable may use reasonable force to execute the writ
- Constable **cannot** execute writ during rain, sleet, or snow
- Landlord is not liable for damages from proper execution of the writ
- Under **SB 38**: If constable does not execute within 3 business days, landlord may request another qualified law enforcement officer

**Your Property After Eviction:**
- The constable/landlord must place your property outside the dwelling in a "reasonably accessible" area
- Landlord is not required to store your property
- You should retrieve belongings promptly

## 2. EVICTION FILING (LANDLORD SIDE)

### 2.1 Required Pre-Suit Notices

**Step 1: Serve a Written Notice to Vacate**

Before filing any eviction suit, you must serve the tenant with proper written notice.

| Situation | Notice Type | Minimum Period |
|---|---|---|
| Nonpayment of rent (tenant paid timely previously) | Notice to Pay Rent or Vacate | 3 days (unless lease says shorter) |
| Nonpayment of rent (general) | Notice to Vacate | 3 days (unless lease says otherwise) |
| Lease violation | Notice to Vacate | 3 days (unless lease says otherwise) |
| Holdover after lease expiration | Notice to Vacate | 3 days (unless lease says otherwise) |
| Month-to-month tenancy termination | Written notice | One full rental period |
| Unauthorized occupant (no lease) | Notice to Vacate | 3 days |

**Notice Delivery Methods (Tex. Prop. Code 24.005):**
1. Personal delivery to tenant or any person residing there who is 16+
2. Placed inside the premises in a conspicuous place (if dwelling is closed)
3. Affixed to the outside of the entry door and sent by first-class mail (if other methods fail)
4. First-class mail, certified mail, registered mail, or delivery service with tracking
5. Email/online portal if tenant agreed in writing (SB 38 addition)

**Best Practice**: Use certified mail with return receipt AND post on the door simultaneously. This creates the strongest evidence trail.

### 2.2 Filing in JP Court (Forcible Entry and Detainer)

**Step 2: File the Eviction Petition**

After the notice period expires (and the tenant has not complied), file in the Justice of the Peace court in the precinct where the property is located.

**Required Information in the Petition:**
- Names and addresses of all parties
- Description of the premises (address)
- Facts entitling plaintiff to possession (lease violation, nonpayment, holdover, etc.)
- Whether tenant missed more than one month's rent (if applicable)
- Whether property is subject to CARES Act
- Copy of the Notice to Vacate with proof of delivery

**Filing Fee**: Varies by county (typically $75-$150)

**Jurisdiction**: JP courts handle evictions (forcible entry and detainer) under Tex. Prop. Code Chapter 24. Monetary damages above $20,000 must be filed separately in a higher court.

**SB 38 Note**: Eviction hearings are now limited to the issue of possession only. Landlords cannot seek unpaid rent as part of the eviction -- that must be a separate lawsuit.

### 2.3 Service Requirements

**Step 3: The Court Serves the Tenant**

The court will issue a citation that must be served on the tenant at least **4 days before the hearing**.

**Service Methods (TRCP Rule 510.4):**
1. Personal delivery by sheriff, constable, or authorized process server
2. Delivery to a person residing at the premises who is 16 years or older
3. If two service attempts fail: mail + notice affixed to the front door (alternative service)

**Important**: Improper service is a valid defense and can result in the case being dismissed.

### 2.4 Trial Process (Landlord Perspective)

**Step 4: Present Your Case at the Hearing (10-21 days after filing)**

The landlord must prove:
1. **Proper notice was given** -- Bring the original notice and proof of delivery (certified mail receipt, photo of posted notice with timestamp, etc.)
2. **Grounds for eviction exist** -- Bring the lease, ledger showing unpaid rent, photos of damage, records of violations
3. **Proper service was completed** -- The court file should show the return of service

**Evidence to Bring:**
- Original lease agreement
- Copy of Notice to Vacate with delivery proof
- Rent ledger showing payment history
- Photos of property damage (if applicable)
- Written correspondence with tenant
- Witness testimony (property manager, maintenance staff)

### 2.5 Writ of Possession

**Step 5: Obtain and Execute the Writ**

If you win:
- Wait 5 days for the appeal period to expire
- If no appeal: Request the Writ of Possession from the court on day 6+
- The constable will post 24-hour notice on the tenant's door
- After 24 hours, the constable executes the writ and removes the tenant
- Under SB 38, constable must execute within 3 business days or landlord may request another officer

**Cost**: Varies by county (typically $150-$300 for constable service)

### 2.6 Collecting Unpaid Rent After Eviction

**Important**: Under Texas law (and reinforced by SB 38), you generally **cannot** recover unpaid rent in the eviction proceeding itself -- only possession.

**Options for Collecting Unpaid Rent:**

1. **Small Claims Court (JP Court)**: File a separate lawsuit for amounts up to $20,000 in the Justice of the Peace court
2. **County/District Court**: For amounts over $20,000
3. **Security Deposit**: Apply the security deposit toward unpaid rent and damages per Tex. Prop. Code 92.104
4. **After Obtaining Judgment**:
   - Request an "execution" from the court clerk authorizing constable to seize eligible property
   - Wage garnishment (limited in Texas)
   - Bank account levy
   - Hire a collection agency
   - Report to credit bureaus

**Statute of Limitations**: You have **4 years** to file a breach of contract claim for unpaid rent (Tex. Civ. Prac. & Rem. Code 16.004).

## 3. SECURITY DEPOSIT DISPUTES

### 3.1 The 30-Day Return Requirement

**Tex. Prop. Code 92.103**: The landlord shall refund a security deposit to the tenant on or before the **30th day** after the date the tenant surrenders the premises.

**Key Rules:**
- The 30-day clock starts when the tenant **surrenders** the premises (returns keys, moves out)
- If the tenant does not provide a written forwarding address, the landlord is not obligated to return the deposit until the address is provided (92.107) -- BUT the landlord is still presumed to act in bad faith if they fail to return within 30 days
- The tenant retains all legal rights to recover the deposit even if they never provided a forwarding address

### 3.2 Itemized Deduction List Requirement

**Tex. Prop. Code 92.104**: If a landlord withholds any portion of the security deposit, they must provide:
- A **written, itemized list** of all damages and charges
- The **cost of repairs** for each item
- The **balance** of the deposit after deductions

**This itemization is required** if the tenant paid all rent in full and there is no rent controversy.

**What Landlords MAY Deduct:**
- Damages beyond normal wear and tear
- Unpaid rent
- Charges the tenant is legally liable for under the lease
- Costs of cleaning beyond what is reasonably expected

**What Landlords MAY NOT Deduct:**
- **Normal wear and tear** (defined as "deterioration that results from the intended use of a dwelling, including breakage or malfunction due to age or deteriorated condition" -- Tex. Prop. Code 92.001(4))
- Examples of normal wear and tear: worn carpet, faded paint, dusty fixtures, minor scuffs
- Examples of deductible damage: holes in walls, broken windows, large stains, excessive filth

### 3.3 Bad Faith Penalties

**Tex. Prop. Code 92.109**: A landlord who **in bad faith** retains a security deposit is liable for:

| Penalty Component | Amount |
|---|---|
| Flat penalty | $100 |
| Wrongfully withheld portion | **3x** the amount wrongfully withheld |
| Attorney's fees | Tenant's reasonable attorney's fees |

**Additional Penalty for Failure to Itemize**: A landlord who in bad faith fails to provide the written itemized list:
1. **Forfeits** the right to withhold any portion of the deposit
2. **Forfeits** the right to sue the tenant for damages to the premises
3. Is liable for the tenant's reasonable attorney's fees

**Presumption of Bad Faith (92.109(d))**: A landlord who fails EITHER to return the deposit OR to provide the written itemization within 30 days is **presumed** to have acted in bad faith.

**Burden of Proof**: In a tenant's lawsuit under this subchapter, the **landlord** has the burden of proving that retaining any portion of the deposit was reasonable.

### 3.4 Small Claims Court Process for Security Deposit Recovery

**Step-by-Step Process:**

1. **Send a Demand Letter**: Before filing suit, send a written demand to the landlord via certified mail requesting return of the deposit. Reference Tex. Prop. Code 92.103-92.109 and the 3x penalty.

2. **Wait a Reasonable Time**: Give the landlord 10-14 days to respond.

3. **File in Justice Court**: Go to the Justice of the Peace court in the precinct where the rental property is located. Claims under $20,000 are heard in JP court.

4. **Filing Fee**: Typically $50-$75 (waivable with Statement of Inability to Pay).

5. **Service**: The court will serve the landlord with the lawsuit.

6. **Hearing**: Present your evidence:
   - Move-in/move-out condition photos
   - Lease agreement
   - Demand letter and certified mail receipt
   - Forwarding address notice (if provided)
   - Evidence of the deposit amount paid
   - Move-out inspection report (if available)

7. **Judgment**: If you prove bad faith, the court awards $100 + 3x the wrongfully withheld amount + attorney's fees.

### 3.5 Monthly Fee Alternative

**Tex. Prop. Code 92.111**: Landlords may offer tenants the option to pay a monthly fee instead of a security deposit. Written disclosure must include:
- The right to pay a traditional security deposit instead
- The right to switch between options at any time
- A comparison of amounts

## 4. REPAIR AND REMEDY

### 4.1 Written Notice Requirements (Tex. Prop. Code 92.056)

**Landlord's Duty**: Under Tex. Prop. Code 92.052, landlords must make diligent efforts to repair conditions that **materially affect the physical health or safety of an ordinary tenant**.

**Examples of Conditions Requiring Repair:**
- Sewage backup/overflow
- Roach or rat infestation
- No hot water
- Faulty wiring
- Roof leaks
- Broken/faulty heating or cooling
- Lack of potable water
- Flooding from broken pipes

**Prerequisites Before Landlord Becomes Liable:**
1. Tenant must be **current on rent** when notice is given
2. Tenant must provide notice to the person or place where rent is normally paid
3. The condition was **not caused** by the tenant, their family, or guests (excluding normal wear and tear)

**Two Notice Procedures:**

**Option A -- Expedited (One Notice):**
- Send initial repair notice via **certified mail, return receipt requested** (or registered mail, or trackable delivery)
- Wait a **reasonable time** (presumed to be 7 days, rebuttable)
- If landlord fails to repair, proceed to remedies

**Option B -- Standard (Two Notices):**
1. Give initial notice (can be verbal or written) to landlord
2. Wait a reasonable time (7-day presumption)
3. If unrepaired, send a **second written notice** requesting repair
4. Wait another reasonable time
5. If still unrepaired, proceed to remedies

**The 7-day presumption is rebuttable** based on: severity of the condition, when the landlord received notice, and reasonable availability of materials and labor.

### 4.2 Landlord's Reasonable Time to Repair

- **Presumption**: 7 days is reasonable (Tex. Prop. Code 92.056)
- **Factors that may extend**: need for specialized parts, contractor availability, severity assessment
- **Factors that may shorten**: emergency conditions (no heat in winter, sewage backup, no water)
- **Landlord Delay Affidavit**: Under 92.0562, a landlord may file an "affidavit for delay" if repairs require more time due to material/labor unavailability

### 4.3 Tenant Remedies (Tex. Prop. Code 92.056(e))

After following proper notice procedures and waiting a reasonable time, tenants have **three statutory remedies** (choose one -- they are alternatives):

**Remedy 1: Court-Ordered Repair (Repair and Remedy Lawsuit) -- 92.0563**
- File a petition in Justice of the Peace court under TRCP Rule 509
- The court may:
  - Order the landlord to take reasonable action to repair
  - Order **rent reduction** (prorated from first repair notice until completion)
  - Award **one month's rent + $500**
  - Award **actual damages**, court costs, and attorney's fees
  - Maximum award: $20,000 (excluding interest and court costs)

**Remedy 2: Repair and Deduct -- 92.0561**
- Tenant arranges repairs and deducts the cost from the next rent payment
- **Strict Requirements:**
  - Only for specific conditions: sewage backup, flooding, broken heating/cooling, no potable water, or conditions certified by local building/health inspector
  - Cost cannot exceed **one month's rent or $500, whichever is greater**
  - Must give landlord written notice describing intended repairs before having them done
  - Must use an **independent, properly licensed contractor** (not the tenant, family, or tenant's employer)
  - Must provide the landlord with the repair bill and payment receipt
  - Cannot use simultaneously with lease termination

**Remedy 3: Lease Termination -- 92.056(e)(1)**
- Tenant may terminate the lease if:
  - All repair notice procedures were completed
  - Condition materially affects health/safety
  - Rent is current
  - Reasonable repair time has passed
- Tenant is entitled to prorated rent refund and security deposit return
- **Caution**: Terminates eligibility for the other two remedies

### 4.4 When Rent Withholding Is Legal (Mostly: It Isn't)

**Texas law does NOT give tenants a general right to withhold rent for failure to repair.**

- Simply refusing to pay rent because the landlord won't repair is **prohibited** under Tex. Prop. Code 92.058
- If a tenant improperly withholds rent, the **landlord** may recover:
  - One month's rent
  - $500 penalty
  - Reasonable attorney's fees
- The tenant's obligation to pay rent and the landlord's duty to repair are treated as **separate obligations** under Texas law

**What You CAN Do Instead:**
- Use the **Repair and Deduct** remedy (92.0561) -- this reduces your rent by the repair cost
- File a **Repair and Remedy lawsuit** (92.0563) seeking court-ordered rent reduction
- Terminate the lease under 92.056(e)(1)

### 4.5 Code Enforcement Complaints

- You may file complaints with your local city or county code enforcement office
- Common agencies: city building inspections, health department, fire marshal
- Filing a code enforcement complaint is a **protected activity** -- the landlord cannot retaliate (92.331)
- An inspector's written determination that a condition affects health/safety can be used as evidence in a Repair and Deduct claim

### 4.6 Constructive Eviction

**Definition**: A constructive eviction occurs when a landlord's actions or omissions make the rental unit uninhabitable, effectively forcing the tenant to leave.

**Elements Required to Prove:**
1. Landlord's intentional act or omission
2. Condition deprives tenant of the use and enjoyment of the premises OR makes the home uninhabitable
3. Tenant actually **vacates** the premises (this is critical -- you must leave to claim constructive eviction)

**Tenant Remedies:**
- Lease termination without liability for future rent
- Actual and special damages
- Potentially punitive damages
- Attorney's fees and court costs

**Practical Steps Before Claiming Constructive Eviction:**
1. Document every condition in writing (photos, videos, dated notes)
2. Send formal repair notice under 92.056
3. Get third-party inspections (code enforcement, independent inspector)
4. Consult an attorney before vacating
5. Secure new housing before moving out
6. Send written notice that you are vacating due to constructive eviction

### 4.7 Landlord Waiver Restrictions

**Tex. Prop. Code 92.006**: A landlord **cannot** waive or modify the duty to repair conditions affecting health/safety except in very narrow circumstances. Lease provisions attempting to waive this duty are **void**.

**Penalty for Illegal Waivers:**
- Actual damages
- One month's rent + $2,000
- Reasonable attorney's fees

## 5. LEASE VIOLATIONS AND DISPUTES

### 5.1 Early Termination Penalties

**General Rule**: A tenant who breaks a lease before expiration remains liable for rent until:
- The lease expires, OR
- A replacement tenant is found

**Landlord's Duty to Mitigate (Tex. Prop. Code 91.006)**:
- The landlord must use **"objectively reasonable efforts"** to find a replacement tenant
- This duty **cannot** be waived in the lease (any such provision is void)
- The landlord need not accept any willing tenant, but must seek "suitable" replacements
- If the landlord fails to mitigate, the tenant's liability is reduced accordingly

**Reletting Fees**:
- Texas permits "reasonable" reletting fees
- Must cover **actual** preparation and administrative costs
- Cannot be punitive or exceed legitimate expenses
- Excessive reletting fees may be challenged in court

### 5.2 Subletting Disputes

**Tex. Prop. Code 91.005**: A tenant may **not** sublease without the landlord's written consent unless the lease expressly permits it.

- If the lease is silent on subletting, the tenant cannot sublet
- The landlord may impose reasonable conditions on subletting
- An unauthorized sublease can be grounds for eviction
- The original tenant generally remains liable under the lease even if subletting is permitted

### 5.3 HOA / Lease Restriction Enforcement

- If the lease incorporates HOA rules, violations of HOA rules may constitute lease violations
- The landlord (not the HOA directly) is typically the party who would pursue eviction
- Tenants should receive a copy of applicable HOA rules at lease signing
- Lease provisions requiring compliance with HOA rules are enforceable

### 5.4 Late Fee Limits (Tex. Prop. Code 92.019)

**Grace Period**: A landlord may not charge a late fee until rent has been unpaid for at least **2 full days** after the due date.

**Reasonable Fee Standards:**

| Property Type | Maximum Presumed Reasonable Fee |
|---|---|
| 1-4 unit structures | **12%** of the monthly rent |
| 5+ unit structures | **10%** of the monthly rent |

- Fees above these percentages may still be lawful if the landlord can prove they represent actual damages from late payment (direct/indirect expenses, collection costs, overhead)
- A late fee may include an **initial fee + daily fee** for each day rent remains unpaid; combined fees are treated as a single late fee
- Lease provisions waiving tenant rights under this section are **void**

**Penalty for Excessive Late Fees:**
- $100
- **3x** the amount of the late fee collected in violation
- Tenant's reasonable attorney's fees

### 5.5 Landlord Entry Rights

**Texas has NO specific statewide statute** governing landlord entry into residential rental property.

**What Controls:**
- **Lease agreement**: Most leases specify when and how the landlord may enter. Follow whatever the lease says.
- **If lease is silent**: Texas courts expect **"reasonable notice"** before entry (commonly interpreted as 24 hours), except in emergencies
- **Emergency entry**: No advance notice required for fires, burst pipes, gas leaks, or similar emergencies
- **Acceptable reasons for entry**: Repairs, inspections, showing to prospective tenants/buyers, emergencies
- **Tenant remedy for unauthorized entry**: Potential claim for breach of quiet enjoyment; may support lease termination in extreme cases

## 6. LOCKOUT PROTECTIONS (Tex. Prop. Code 92.0081)

### 6.1 When a Landlord May Temporarily Change Locks

A landlord may change the locks on a delinquent tenant's unit ONLY if **ALL** of these conditions are met:

1. The **lease** specifically includes notice of the landlord's right to change locks for delinquent rent
2. Tenant is delinquent on at least part of the rent
3. Landlord provides **advance written notice**:
   - By mail: not later than the **5th calendar day** before the lock change
   - By hand delivery or door posting: not later than the **3rd calendar day** before
4. The notice states:
   - Earliest date of the proposed lock change
   - Amount of rent required to prevent the change
   - Where rent can be paid during business hours
   - In **bold or underlined print**: tenant's right to receive a key at any hour regardless of whether rent is paid
5. **No one is inside the dwelling** at the time of the lock change
6. Locks have not already been changed during the current rental payment period

### 6.2 Required Notice at Time of Lockout

If a landlord changes the locks, they **must** post written notice on the tenant's front door stating:
- An on-site location where the tenant may go **24 hours a day** to obtain the new key, OR a telephone number answered **24 hours a day** to have a key delivered within 2 hours
- The landlord **must** provide the new key at any hour **regardless of whether the tenant pays delinquent rent**
- The amount of delinquent rent

### 6.3 What Landlords Cannot Do

- Change locks when someone is inside the dwelling
- Change locks more than once per rental payment period
- Remove doors, windows, locks, latches, hinges, doorknobs, or other mechanisms
- Remove landlord-supplied furniture, fixtures, or appliances (except for repair/replacement)
- Permanently lock out a tenant (this is not a substitute for eviction)

### 6.4 Tenant Remedies for Unlawful Lockout (Tex. Prop. Code 92.009)

If a landlord violates lockout rules, the tenant may file in Justice Court for:

1. **Writ of Re-Entry**: Court orders the landlord to restore access
2. **Lease Termination**: Tenant may choose to terminate the lease instead

**Damages Available:**
- One month's rent
- $1,000
- Court costs
- Actual damages
- Reasonable attorney's fees

**Additional Penalty**: If the landlord fails to provide a key as required, the tenant may recover an additional civil penalty of **one month's rent**.

## 7. UTILITY SHUTOFF PROTECTIONS (Tex. Prop. Code 92.008)

### 7.1 What Is Prohibited

A landlord or landlord's agent **may not** interrupt or cause the interruption of:
- Utility service paid for **directly by the tenant** to the utility company
- Water, wastewater, gas, or electric service **furnished by the landlord** to the tenant

**Exceptions**: Interruption is permitted only for:
- Bona fide repairs
- Construction
- Emergency

**Deliberate shutoff of utilities as a form of eviction or punishment for nonpayment is strictly illegal.**

### 7.2 Tenant Remedies for Utility Shutoff

If a landlord violates this section, the tenant may:

**Option A -- Recover Possession or Terminate Lease**, PLUS recover:
- Actual damages
- One month's rent + $1,000
- Reasonable attorney's fees
- Court costs

**Option B -- Emergency Writ of Restoration:**
1. File a **sworn complaint** with Justice Court in the precinct where the property is located
2. State the facts orally under oath to the justice
3. If the justice reasonably believes an unlawful disconnection occurred, the court may issue a **Writ of Restoration of Utility Service**
4. This entitles the tenant to **immediate and temporary** restoration of the disconnected utility, pending a final hearing

## 8. SPECIAL LEASE TERMINATION RIGHTS

Texas law provides several statutory rights to terminate a lease early without liability:

### 8.1 Family Violence (Tex. Prop. Code 92.016)

- Survivors of family violence (as defined by Tex. Fam. Code 71.004) may terminate without liability for future rent
- Requires **30 days' written notice** + documentation of abuse (protective order, police report, etc.)

### 8.2 Sexual Assault or Stalking (Tex. Prop. Code 92.0161)

- Victims of sexual offenses or stalking (and parents/guardians of minor victims) may terminate
- Abuse must have occurred within the previous **6 months**
- Requires **30 days' written notice** + documentation

### 8.3 Military Service (Tex. Prop. Code 92.017)

- Active-duty servicemembers, dependents, and individuals entering military service after lease signing
- Applicable for deployment lasting **90+ days** or permanent restationing
- Requires written notice + copy of military orders

### 8.4 Tenant Death (Tex. Prop. Code 92.0162)

- Estate representatives of a deceased sole-occupant tenant may terminate via written notice

### 8.5 Landlord Failure to Repair (Tex. Prop. Code 92.056)

- After completing repair notice procedures and waiting reasonable time
- See Section 4.3, Remedy 3 above

### 8.6 Smoke Alarm Non-Compliance (Tex. Prop. Code 92.259)

- If landlord fails to install, inspect, or repair smoke alarms after written request
- **7-day grace period** before lease terminates

### 8.7 Missing Management Disclosure (Tex. Prop. Code 92.205)

- If landlord fails to disclose or provides false information about property ownership/management
- **7-day notice** period before termination

### 8.8 Public Indecency Conviction (Tex. Prop. Code 91.003)

- Landlord may terminate lease if tenant is convicted under Tex. Penal Code Chapter 43 and has exhausted direct appeals
- Applies only to leases executed or renewed after June 15, 1981

## SB 38 SUMMARY (Effective January 1, 2026)

Key changes from Senate Bill 38 (89th Texas Legislature) that affect cases filed on or after January 1, 2026:

| Change | Impact |
|---|---|
| **Eviction hearings limited to possession only** | No counterclaims; monetary disputes must be separate lawsuits |
| **Summary Disposition process** | Faster resolution if tenant does not respond within 4 days; judgment possible in as few as 5 days |
| **Electronic notice permitted** | Email/online portal notice acceptable if tenant agreed in writing |
| **Good faith oath strengthened** | Tenants appealing must swear under penalty of perjury that appeal is not for delay |
| **Constable execution deadline** | 3 business days to execute writ; if missed, landlord may use other law enforcement |
| **Chapter 24B -- Unauthorized Occupant Removal** | Non-court expedited process for removing squatters via sheriff/constable complaint |
| **Tenant protections preserved** | Written notice, service, right to respond, appeal rights, and military protections remain intact |
