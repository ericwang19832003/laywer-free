## 1. DEBT VALIDATION RIGHTS

### A. FDCPA Section 1692g — Validation of Debts

**Initial Notice Requirements (15 U.S.C. § 1692g(a))**

Within **5 days** of initial communication regarding debt collection, a debt collector must send a written notice ("validation notice") containing:

1. **The amount of the debt**
2. **The name of the creditor** to whom the debt is owed
3. **A statement** that unless the consumer disputes the debt within 30 days, it will be assumed valid
4. **A statement** that if the consumer disputes in writing within 30 days, the collector will obtain and mail verification of the debt or a copy of a judgment
5. **A statement** that upon written request within 30 days, the collector will provide the name and address of the original creditor (if different from current creditor)

**The 30-Day Dispute Window (§ 1692g(a)-(b))**

- You have **30 days from receipt** of the validation notice to dispute the debt **in writing**
- If you dispute within 30 days, the collector **must cease all collection activity** until they obtain verification and mail it to you
- Collection activities may continue during the 30-day period *only if* you have not yet sent a written dispute
- The collector's communications during this period must not "overshadow or be inconsistent with" your right to dispute

**What Happens If You Don't Dispute (§ 1692g(c))**

- Failure to dispute does **NOT** constitute an admission of liability in any legal proceeding
- The debt is merely "assumed valid" by the collector — this has no legal effect in court

**Legal Pleadings Exception (§ 1692g(d))**

- A formal civil complaint/lawsuit does not count as an "initial communication" triggering validation notice requirements

### B. What the Collector Must Provide Upon Validation Request

The FDCPA does not prescribe an exact list of required documents, but courts have held that "verification" must be sufficient to confirm:

- The debt is valid and belongs to you
- The amount claimed is correct

Adequate verification typically includes:
- **Account statements** from the original creditor
- **Chain of ownership documentation** if the debt was sold/assigned
- **Itemized breakdown** of principal, interest, fees, and charges
- **Copy of the original signed agreement** or contract
- **Confirmation of the balance** including any accrued fees or interest

**Important**: A form letter restating the same amount without supporting documentation is **not** sufficient verification.

### C. Effect of Requesting Validation

Once you send a written dispute within the 30-day window:

1. **All collection activity must stop** — phone calls, letters, credit reporting
2. The collector must obtain verification of the debt
3. The collector must mail verification to you
4. Collection may only resume **after** verification is mailed
5. If the collector cannot verify, they cannot continue collection

### D. Texas Debt Collection Act (TDCPA) — Additional State Protections

The TDCPA (Texas Finance Code Chapter 392) provides **broader protections** than the FDCPA:

**Key Differences from FDCPA:**
- Applies to **original creditors** as well as third-party collectors (FDCPA only covers third-party collectors)
- Covers any person "directly or indirectly" engaging in debt collection
- Includes entities "selling collection systems"
- No statutory exceptions for mortgage servicers (unlike FDCPA)

**Pre-Lawsuit Requirements Under Texas Law:**
- Before suing, the creditor must send a **written demand letter at least 30 days before filing**
- You have **30 days** to dispute via written reply

**TDCPA Prohibited Practices (Tex. Fin. Code § 392.301-392.304):**
- Threats, coercion, harassment, or abuse
- Fraudulent, deceptive, or misleading representations
- Unfair or unconscionable collection means
- Claiming nonpayment is criminal or threatening jail
- Threatening to collect from third parties (family members)
- Threatening seizure of homestead or wages
- Calling outside 8 AM - 9 PM (unless you consent)
- Contacting you after learning an attorney represents you
- Collecting unauthorized amounts after written notice and police report

### E. What a Proper Debt Validation Letter Should Contain

**Your letter should include:**

1. Your name and address (but no SSN or unnecessary personal information)
2. The account number or reference number from the collector's notice
3. Explicit citation to **15 U.S.C. § 1692g**
4. A clear statement that you are disputing the debt
5. A request for specific documentation:
   - Original creditor's name and address
   - Exact amount with itemized breakdown (principal, interest, fees)
   - Copy of original signed agreement/contract
   - Proof you are the correct debtor
   - Proof the collector is authorized to collect
   - Proof the collector is licensed in Texas
6. A statement that you do **not** acknowledge owing the debt (use "alleged debt" throughout)

**Best Practices:**
- Send via **USPS Certified Mail with Return Receipt Requested** (~$10-11)
- Keep copies of everything
- Never acknowledge owing the debt in any communication
- Track the 30-day deadline carefully
- Retain the green return receipt card as proof of delivery

## 2. TEXAS PROPERTY EXEMPTIONS

### A. Homestead Exemption (Texas Property Code Chapter 41)

Texas provides one of the strongest homestead protections in the country:

**Urban Homestead:**
- Up to **10 acres** (single person or family)
- **No cap on value** — Texas has an unlimited value homestead exemption
- Must be your primary residence

**Rural Homestead:**
- Single person: up to **100 acres**
- Family: up to **200 acres**
- No cap on value

**Homestead Sale Proceeds:**
- Proceeds from selling your homestead remain **exempt for 6 months** after the date of sale

**Exceptions — Homestead CAN be seized for:**
- Mortgage/deed of trust (purchase money)
- Property tax liens
- Home equity loan payments
- Mechanic's liens (home improvement)
- Owelty of partition liens

### B. Personal Property Exemptions (Texas Property Code §§ 42.001-42.002)

**Aggregate Value Limits:**
- Single adult: **$50,000** total fair market value
- Family: **$100,000** total fair market value
- These limits apply to the combined value of all personal property categories below (excluding items with no value cap)

**Protected Categories (§ 42.002):**

*Items with NO value cap (exempt regardless of value):*
- **One motor vehicle per licensed family member**
- **Health aids** (professionally prescribed)
- **Sacred religious texts**

*Items subject to the aggregate cap:*
- **Home furnishings** — furniture, family heirlooms necessary for daily living
- **Provisions** — food and household consumables
- **Farming/ranching equipment** — vehicles and implements
- **Tools of trade** — tools, equipment, books, apparatus (including boats and motor vehicles used in a trade or profession)
- **Clothing**
- **Jewelry** — up to **$12,500** (single) / **$25,000** (family)
- **Two firearms**
- **Specified livestock** — including 12 cattle, horses (2), and other animals
- **Household pets**
- **Athletic and sporting equipment**
- **Bicycles**

### C. Wage Exemptions

**Current Wages — 100% Exempt:**
- Under Texas Property Code § 42.001(b)(1), current wages for personal services are **completely exempt** from "garnishment, attachment, execution, and other seizure"
- Wages are expressly **excluded from the $50,000/$100,000 aggregate cap**
- Your paycheck **cannot be garnished from your employer** for consumer debt judgments

**Exceptions — Wages CAN be garnished for:**
- Court-ordered child support
- Court-ordered spousal maintenance/alimony
- Federally guaranteed student loans in default
- Federal income taxes (IRS)

**Critical Bank Account Rule:**
- Once deposited into a bank account, wages **lose their "current wages" protection** and become subject to garnishment
- **Strategy**: Keep wages in a separate account; do NOT commingle with other funds

**Federal Benefits Two-Month Rule:**
- If Social Security or other federal benefits are **directly deposited**, the bank must **automatically protect two months' worth** of those benefits
- Even if other money in the account is frozen, these two months of benefits must remain accessible
- If deposited by check (not direct deposit), you can still claim this exemption but must prove the source

### D. Retirement Accounts — Fully Exempt

Protected accounts include:
- **401(k) plans**
- **403(b) plans**
- **Traditional IRAs**
- **Roth IRAs**
- **Pension and profit-sharing plans**
- **Government retirement accounts**
- **Inherited retirement accounts** (Texas protects these, unlike some states)

**No dollar limit** on the amount protected in retirement accounts in Texas.

**Exceptions:**
- IRS can levy for unpaid federal taxes
- Court-ordered child support/spousal maintenance
- Fraudulent contributions (intent to hide assets from creditors)

### E. Insurance and Annuities — Exempt

- **Life insurance cash value and death benefits**
- **Annuity benefits**
- **Health and accident insurance benefits**
- **529 college savings plans**
- **Prepaid tuition plans**

Protections may depend on policy structure and named beneficiaries.

### F. Other Exempt Income

- Social Security (retirement, SSI, SSDI)
- Veterans Administration benefits
- Railroad Retirement Board benefits
- Workers' compensation benefits
- Unemployment benefits
- TANF (welfare) funds
- FEMA disaster benefits
- Court-ordered child support and spousal support received
- **Unpaid commissions** — up to $12,500 (single) / $25,000 (family)

### G. How to Claim Exemptions After Judgment

**When Seizure Occurs:**
1. You should receive a **Seizure Exemption Notice**, **Instructions**, and a **Seizure Exemption Claim Form** from the receiver or judgment creditor
2. If you do not receive these forms within **5 days** of property being frozen, contact the court immediately

**Filing Process:**
1. **Complete the Protected Property Claim Form** — check all boxes for exempt property that was frozen/taken
2. **Make copies** for your records and each interested party
3. **File with the court** (listed at top of form) — by mail, in person, or eFile (mark as containing sensitive data)
4. **Serve notice** — same day you file, mail or email copies to all persons listed in "Interested Persons to Notify" section
5. **Sign the Certificate of Service** — court may refuse to return property without this
6. **Attend the hearing** — court should schedule within **10 days** of receiving your claim

**Deadline: File within 14 days** — after that, exempt property may be sold or released to the creditor.

**Evidence to Bring to Hearing:**
- Pay stubs
- Bank account statements showing source of deposits
- Government agency benefit letters
- Divorce decrees (for support payments)
- Vehicle titles
- Employment records
- Retirement account statements

**Critical Strategy: Keep Exempt and Non-Exempt Funds Separate**
- If exempt funds are mixed with non-exempt funds, you bear the burden of proving which funds came from exempt sources
- Keeping a dedicated account for exempt income (Social Security, wages, etc.) dramatically simplifies the claims process
- File an **anti-garnishment letter** with your bank proactively

## 3. STATUTE OF LIMITATIONS

### A. Limitation Periods by Debt Type

| Debt Type | Period | Citation |
|-----------|--------|----------|
| Written contracts | **4 years** | Tex. Civ. Prac. & Rem. Code § 16.004(a)(3) |
| Oral agreements | **4 years** | Tex. Civ. Prac. & Rem. Code § 16.004(a)(3) |
| Open accounts (credit cards) | **4 years** | Tex. Civ. Prac. & Rem. Code § 16.004(a)(3) |
| Promissory notes (non-negotiable) | **4 years** | Tex. Civ. Prac. & Rem. Code § 16.004(a)(3) |
| Promissory notes (negotiable instruments) | **6 years** | Tex. Bus. & Com. Code § 3.118 |
| Judgments | **10 years** (renewable) | Tex. Civ. Prac. & Rem. Code § 34.001 |

**Promissory Note Distinction:**
- If the note qualifies as a **negotiable instrument** under the UCC, the **6-year** period applies (measured from the due date stated in the note)
- If the note is **non-negotiable**, the standard **4-year** period applies
- Analysis of the note's terms is required to determine which applies

### B. When the Clock Starts

The statute of limitations generally begins running from:

1. **Date of first nonpayment/breach** — the most common trigger
2. **Date of last payment** — in some circumstances
3. **Date of last charge** — for open accounts, sometimes from the last transaction
4. **Acceleration date** — for installment contracts where the creditor accelerates the entire balance

The precise trigger depends on the type of debt and the contract terms. For credit card debt, it typically starts from the date of the first missed payment that was never cured.

### C. Effect of Partial Payment on the Statute of Limitations

**2019 Texas Reform — Critical Change:**

Texas Finance Code § 392.307 (effective 2019) fundamentally changed the rules for **debt buyers**:

- **Payments do NOT restart the clock** for debts held by debt buyers
- **Acknowledging the debt does NOT restart the clock** for debt buyers
- Debt buyers are **prohibited from suing** after the statute expires, even if payments were made

**For Original Creditors (pre-2019 common law may still apply):**
- A **written acknowledgment** of the debt may revive the limitations period
- Signing a **new promissory note** or entering a **new agreement** to pay can restart the clock (essentially a new contract)
- A mere partial payment, standing alone, is debatable — consult an attorney

**Warning**: Be very careful about:
- Making any payment on old debt
- Acknowledging the debt in writing
- Signing any new agreement or payment plan
- Any of these actions could potentially restart the clock (for original creditor debts)

### D. SOL as a Defense vs. Credit Reporting Time

These are two **completely separate** concepts:

| | Statute of Limitations | Credit Reporting Period |
|---|---|---|
| **What it governs** | Right to **sue** you | Right to **report** on credit |
| **Duration** | 4 years (most TX debts) | **7 years** from first delinquency |
| **Governed by** | State law (TX Civ. Prac. & Rem. Code) | Federal law (Fair Credit Reporting Act) |
| **Effect of expiration** | Cannot sue; affirmative defense | Must remove from credit report |
| **Restarted by payment** | Possibly (see above) | **Never** — 7-year clock is fixed from original delinquency date |

**Key Points:**
- A debt can be **time-barred** (SOL expired) but still appear on your credit report
- A debt can be **off your credit report** but still within the SOL
- You still legally "owe" a time-barred debt — the creditor just cannot sue to collect it
- SOL is an **affirmative defense** — you must raise it; the court will not raise it for you

### E. How to Raise the SOL Defense

- You must assert it in your **Answer** to the lawsuit as an affirmative defense
- Cite **Tex. Civ. Prac. & Rem. Code § 16.004** (or applicable section)
- State that the plaintiff's claims are barred by the applicable statute of limitations
- The burden then shifts to the plaintiff to prove the claim is timely

### F. Federal Protection for Time-Barred Debts

Under **12 C.F.R. § 1006.26**, debt collectors are prohibited from:
- Suing or threatening to sue on time-barred debts
- This is both an FDCPA violation and a CFPB regulation violation

## 4. FDCPA / TDCPA COUNTERCLAIMS

### A. FDCPA Violations That Give Rise to Counterclaims

**Harassment or Abuse (15 U.S.C. § 1692d):**
- Use or threat of violence or criminal means
- Use of obscene or profane language
- Publishing lists of consumers who refuse to pay (except to credit bureaus)
- Calling without meaningful disclosure of caller's identity
- Causing a telephone to ring continuously with intent to annoy or harass
- Repeated calls intended to annoy, abuse, or harass

**False or Misleading Representations (15 U.S.C. § 1692e):**
- Falsely representing the amount, character, or legal status of the debt
- Falsely claiming to be an attorney or government representative
- Threatening actions that cannot legally be taken or are not intended
- False representation that nonpayment will result in arrest or imprisonment
- Communicating false credit information (including failing to report a dispute)
- Using false or deceptive means to collect (e.g., fake legal documents)
- Falsely representing that sale/transfer of debt causes loss of defenses
- Misrepresenting the amount owed

**Unfair Practices (15 U.S.C. § 1692f):**
- Collecting any amount not authorized by the agreement or law (unauthorized fees/interest)
- Accepting post-dated checks more than 5 days early
- Threatening to deposit post-dated checks early
- Causing collection charges (telegrams, etc.) to disguise the purpose
- Taking or threatening nonjudicial action on exempt property
- Communicating via postcard (privacy violation)
- Using symbols on envelopes indicating debt collection

**Validation Violations (15 U.S.C. § 1692g):**
- Failing to send the required validation notice within 5 days
- Continuing collection after written dispute without providing verification
- Overshadowing or contradicting the validation notice with collection demands

**Communication Violations (15 U.S.C. § 1692c):**
- Contacting you before 8 AM or after 9 PM
- Contacting you at work after being told employer prohibits it
- Contacting you directly when represented by an attorney
- Contacting third parties about your debt (except limited exceptions)
- Continuing contact after receiving written cease-and-desist

### B. Statutory Damages Available

**FDCPA (15 U.S.C. § 1692k):**
- **Actual damages** — emotional distress, lost wages, medical bills, etc.
- **Statutory damages** — up to **$1,000 per case** (not per violation)
- **Attorney's fees and costs** — reasonable fees if you prevail
- **Class actions** — up to the lesser of $500,000 or 1% of the debt collector's net worth

**TDCPA (Tex. Fin. Code § 392.403):**
- **Injunctive relief** — court order to stop the behavior
- **Actual damages** — proven losses
- **Minimum $100 per violation** for specific sections (§ 392.101 bonding, § 392.202, § 392.301(a)(3))
- **Attorney's fees** reasonably related to work performed, plus costs
- If your suit is found to be **bad faith**, the defendant recovers their attorney's fees

**Criminal Penalties (TDCPA § 392.402):**
- TDCPA violations are **misdemeanors**: fines of $100 to $500 per violation
- Criminal charges must be filed within one year

### C. Texas DTPA Violations in Debt Collection

TDCPA violations automatically constitute **deceptive trade practices** under the Texas Deceptive Trade Practices-Consumer Protection Act (Tex. Bus. & Com. Code § 17, Subchapter E), per Tex. Fin. Code § 392.404.

**DTPA Damages:**
- **Economic damages** (actual losses)
- **Mental anguish damages**
- If the court finds the defendant acted **"knowingly"**: up to **3x economic damages** (treble damages)
- If the court finds the defendant acted **"intentionally"**: up to **3x mental anguish damages** as well
- **Attorney's fees and court costs** for prevailing consumer

**DTPA Notice Requirement:**
- You must send **written notice** of the DTPA claim to the defendant at least **60 days before filing suit**
- This gives them an opportunity to settle

### D. Attorney Fees Recovery

Both FDCPA and TDCPA provide for recovery of reasonable attorney's fees:
- FDCPA: Awarded to prevailing consumer plaintiff
- TDCPA: Fees "reasonably related to work performed" plus costs
- DTPA: "Reasonable and necessary attorneys' fees" for prevailing consumer
- Even pro se litigants may recover costs (but attorney's fees require an attorney)

### E. Common Violations to Watch For

1. **Suing on time-barred debt** — violation of FDCPA and 12 C.F.R. § 1006.26
2. **Failing to send validation notice** within 5 days of first contact
3. **Continuing collection after written dispute** without providing verification
4. **Calling excessively** — multiple calls per day, calling at prohibited times
5. **Threatening arrest or jail** for nonpayment of consumer debt
6. **Misrepresenting the debt amount** — adding unauthorized fees or interest
7. **Threatening to garnish wages** (mostly prohibited in Texas)
8. **Threatening to take homestead** (exempt in Texas)
9. **Contacting third parties** (family, employer, neighbors) about the debt
10. **Failing to identify themselves** as debt collectors in communications
11. **Filing suit without proper documentation** of standing to collect
12. **Failing to maintain $10,000 surety bond** with TX Secretary of State (TDCPA § 392.101)

### F. How to File Counterclaims

If you are sued by a third-party debt collector or debt collection law firm that has committed FDCPA/TDCPA violations, you can:

1. **File a counterclaim** in the existing lawsuit — this is often the simplest approach
2. **File a separate lawsuit** against the collector
3. The right to removal to federal court does **not** apply when the consumer invokes the FDCPA for purposes of a counterclaim in a pending state court debt collection suit

**TDCPA Statute of Limitations for Claims:**
- At least **2 years**, arguably **4 years** from the violation
- FDCPA statute of limitations: **1 year** from the violation
- Strategic advantage: TDCPA's longer window means state claims may survive even when federal claims are time-barred

## 5. POST-JUDGMENT PROCEDURES

### A. Abstract of Judgment — Creates Lien

- After obtaining a judgment, a creditor can file an **Abstract of Judgment** with the county clerk
- This creates a **lien on all non-exempt real property** you own in that county
- The creditor can file abstracts in **multiple counties**
- The lien lasts as long as the judgment is enforceable (**10 years**, renewable)
- A judgment lien does **not** attach to your homestead

### B. Turnover Orders

- A court can issue a **turnover order** compelling you to turn over non-exempt property to satisfy the judgment
- The court may appoint a **turnover receiver** — a third party authorized to:
  - Locate and take possession of your property
  - Access multiple accounts and property in one action
  - Sell non-exempt property to satisfy the debt
- Turnover receivers have broader powers than garnishment (which targets one account at a time)

### C. Bank Account Garnishment Process

**How It Works:**
1. Creditor obtains judgment
2. Creditor files a **separate garnishment action** (a new lawsuit) against the bank
3. Court issues a **Writ of Garnishment** to the bank
4. Bank **freezes your account immediately** upon receiving the writ — often before you receive notice
5. **No advance notice** is given to you before the freeze
6. The bank responds to the court confirming account ownership and balance

**What Gets Frozen:**
- If your balance is **less than** the judgment amount: **entire account** is frozen
- If your balance **exceeds** the judgment amount: only the judgment amount (plus interest/fees) is frozen; remainder is accessible

**Timeline:**
- The garnishment process operates as a separate lawsuit and can take several months
- Judgments remain enforceable for **10 years** and accrue interest
- Judgments can be **renewed** for additional periods

**Your Response Options:**
1. **File a Protected Property Claim Form** — if exempt money was frozen (within 14 days)
2. **File a Motion to Dissolve or Modify** — if you dispute the debt amount, weren't properly served, or other grounds exist
3. **Post a Replevy Bond** — recover property by posting equal-value security (rarely used; consult attorney)
4. **Negotiate a payment plan** — even after garnishment, you can negotiate with the creditor

### D. Motion to Set Aside Default Judgment — The Craddock Test

If you received a default judgment (because you failed to file an answer or appear), you may be able to have it set aside.

**The Craddock Test** (*Craddock v. Sunshine Bus Lines, Inc.*):

You must prove **all three elements**:

1. **Your failure to respond was not intentional or due to conscious indifference**
   - You didn't deliberately ignore the lawsuit
   - Examples: never actually received service, illness, confusion about deadlines, military deployment
   - "Conscious indifference" means you knew about the suit but didn't care enough to respond

2. **You have a meritorious defense** to the plaintiff's claim
   - You don't have to prove you'll win — just that you have a legitimate defense
   - Examples: statute of limitations expired, debt was already paid, wrong person, amount is incorrect, lack of standing

3. **Setting aside the judgment will not cause undue delay or prejudice** to the opposing party
   - This element is usually the easiest to meet
   - Simply resuming the case on its merits does not typically cause prejudice

**Filing Deadlines:**

| Court Type | Deadline |
|-----------|----------|
| Justice Court | **14 days** after judgment was signed |
| County or District Court | **30 days** after judgment was signed |

**Extended Timeline Exception:**
- If you discovered the judgment **more than 20 days** after it was signed, your deadline begins when you received notice
- Maximum filing window: **90 days** from the date the default judgment was signed (absolute outer limit)
- Active duty military members receive additional time

**Bill of Review:**
- If you miss all the above deadlines, you may file a **Bill of Review** — an independent action to set aside the judgment
- This is a more difficult standard requiring proof of:
  - A meritorious defense
  - That you were prevented from making the defense by fraud, accident, or wrongful act of the opposing party (or official mistake)
  - No fault or negligence on your part

### E. Payment Plans / Agreed Judgments

- You can negotiate a **payment plan** with the creditor at any point, even after judgment
- Any agreement should be **put in writing** and filed with the court as an **Agreed Judgment** or **Settlement Agreement**
- Understand the total cost including accrued interest before agreeing
- If all your income and property is exempt, you may be **"judgment proof"** and negotiation may not be necessary

### F. Post-Judgment Discovery

After waiting **30 days from judgment**, creditors can send discovery requiring you to disclose assets:

- **Interrogatories** — written questions about your assets (bank accounts, financial information, employment)
- **Requests for Admissions** — statements you must admit or deny
- **Requests for Production** — demands for documents (bank statements, pay stubs, tax returns)

**Response deadline:** Typically **30 days** from receipt.

**Critical Warning:** Failure to respond to Requests for Admissions results in the matters being deemed **automatically admitted**, which can cause you to lose critical protections.

**Response deadline for all discovery in the original suit:** **50 days** from receipt.

### G. Judgment Duration and Renewal

- Judgments are enforceable for **10 years** (for non-government creditors)
- Judgments can be **renewed** for additional periods
- **Revival window:** Generally 2 years to attempt reviving dormant judgments
- Judgments accrue **post-judgment interest** (currently set by statute)
- Judgments may remain on credit reports for up to **10 years** (though some agencies remove after 7)

## KEY STRATEGIC CONSIDERATIONS

### Are You "Judgment Proof"?

If **all** of your income and assets fall within Texas exemptions, you may be effectively "judgment proof" — meaning even if the creditor wins a judgment, there is nothing they can legally seize. This applies if:

- Your only income is Social Security, VA benefits, retirement, or other exempt sources
- Your only real property is your homestead
- Your personal property falls within the $50,000/$100,000 caps
- You have no non-exempt bank account balances

Being judgment proof does not mean the lawsuit goes away — the judgment still exists, accrues interest, and affects your credit. But as a practical matter, the creditor cannot collect.

### Responding to a Lawsuit — Critical Deadlines

| Court Type | Answer Deadline |
|-----------|----------------|
| District/County Court | **Monday following 20 days** from service |
| Justice Court | **14 days** from service |

**Failure to answer = default judgment.** Always file an answer, even if you believe the debt is valid.

### Five Core Defenses to Assert

1. **Statute of limitations** — the 4-year SOL has expired
2. **Lack of standing** — plaintiff cannot prove they own the debt (demand chain of title)
3. **Insufficient documentation** — plaintiff cannot produce the original agreement or account statements
4. **Wrong amount** — the claimed amount includes unauthorized fees, interest, or charges
5. **Wrong person** — identity theft, mistaken identity, or wrong account
