## 1. When You Receive a Citation/Petition

### What You Will Receive

When a creditor or debt buyer sues you, you will be served with:
- **Citation** — official court notice telling you that you have been sued and your deadline to respond
- **Petition** — the plaintiff's complaint describing the debt, amount claimed, and legal basis for the suit

### Critical Deadlines

| Court Type | Answer Deadline |
|---|---|
| **Justice Court** (small claims, up to $20,000) | **14 days** after the date of service |
| **County Court at Law** | **Monday following 20 days** after the date of service (the "answer Monday" rule) |
| **District Court** | **Monday following 20 days** after the date of service |

- If the deadline falls on a weekend or court holiday, you have until the next business day.
- Use the Answer Deadline Calculator on TexasLawHelp.org to determine your exact due date.

### Immediate Action Steps

1. **Read every word** of the citation and petition — note the case number, court, plaintiff, plaintiff's attorney, amount claimed, and the account they claim is yours.
2. **Do NOT ignore the lawsuit.** If you fail to answer by the deadline, the court will enter a **default judgment** against you.
3. **Gather documents** — credit card statements, bills, receipts, payment records, letters, emails, and any evidence that challenges the debt or supports your defenses.
4. **Check the statute of limitations** — if the debt is older than 4 years from the last payment or breach, you may have a complete defense.
5. **Check whether the plaintiff is the original creditor or a debt buyer** — debt buyers often lack proper documentation to prove they own your specific account.

## 2. Filing Your Answer

### What Is an Answer?

Your Answer is the legal document you file with the court responding to the plaintiff's petition. It preserves your right to defend yourself at trial.

### Types of Answers

#### General Denial
A general denial does **not** claim the plaintiff is wrong. It simply requires the plaintiff to **prove every element of their case**. In Texas, a general denial is powerful because the burden of proof stays entirely on the plaintiff.

Example language:
> "Defendant generally denies each and every, all and singular, the allegations contained in Plaintiff's Original Petition, and demands strict proof thereof."

#### Specific Denials
You can also deny specific allegations — for example:
- "Defendant denies that the amount claimed is correct."
- "Defendant denies that Plaintiff is the holder of the account."
- "Defendant denies that Defendant entered into any agreement with Plaintiff."

#### Best Practice: Combine Both
File a **general denial** (catches everything) PLUS **specific denials and affirmative defenses** for your strongest arguments.

### How to File

**Texas Justice Court (Form CV-ANS-103):**
1. Copy the case number, county, precinct, and place number exactly from your lawsuit papers.
2. Enter your name as defendant.
3. Check boxes for applicable defenses and protected assets.
4. Indicate whether you want a jury trial ($22 fee, must be requested at least 14 days before trial).
5. Sign the form (typed signature with "/s/" prefix is acceptable).
6. Complete the Certificate of Service on the last page.

**Filing Methods:**
- In-person at the courthouse clerk's office
- Electronic filing via eFileTexas.gov
- By email (confirm with clerk first)
- By mail — use **Registered Mail, Return Receipt Requested**

**You MUST also serve a copy on the plaintiff or their attorney** using the method specified in your Certificate of Service.

### Online Tools

- **Lone Star Legal Aid** provides an interactive online tool to generate your Answer for Justice Court debt cases at TexasLawHelp.org.
- **eFileTexas.gov** has guided interviews for creating court filings.
- **Texas Justice Court Training Center** provides sample answer forms.

## 3. Affirmative Defenses Available in Texas

An affirmative defense is a legal reason the plaintiff should not win **even if what they allege is true**. You **must** assert affirmative defenses in your Answer or you risk waiving them permanently.

### Key Affirmative Defenses in Debt Cases

#### 3.1 Statute of Limitations (Tex. Civ. Prac. & Rem. Code § 16.004)
- The plaintiff filed suit **more than 4 years** after the cause of action accrued (typically the date of last payment or breach).
- This is an **absolute bar** to recovery if proven.

#### 3.2 Lack of Standing / Plaintiff Cannot Prove Ownership
- The plaintiff (especially a debt buyer) cannot prove an unbroken **chain of assignment** from the original creditor to themselves.
- The plaintiff cannot produce the **original signed agreement** between you and the original creditor.
- The account numbers, amounts, or names don't match.

#### 3.3 Discharge in Bankruptcy
- The debt was included in a bankruptcy filing and a **discharge order** was entered by the bankruptcy court.
- Provide a copy of the discharge order.

#### 3.4 Accord and Satisfaction
- You and the creditor previously agreed to settle the debt for a lesser amount, and you paid that amount.
- The creditor accepted the payment and cannot now sue for the remainder.

#### 3.5 Payment / Already Paid
- The debt has already been paid in full or the amount claimed is incorrect.
- Provide proof of payment (receipts, bank statements, canceled checks).

#### 3.6 Wrong Amount
- The amount the plaintiff claims does not match what is actually owed.
- Interest, fees, or charges have been improperly calculated.

#### 3.7 Identity Theft / Not My Debt
- The debt was incurred through identity theft and is not yours.
- Under Texas Finance Code § 392.308, once you obtain a court order declaring victim status, the creditor must cease collection.
- Supporting evidence: police report, FTC Identity Theft Report, credit monitoring alerts.

#### 3.8 Statute of Frauds
- Certain contracts (e.g., sales of goods over $500) must be in writing to be enforceable (Tex. Bus. & Com. Code § 26.01).
- If the plaintiff cannot produce a written, signed agreement, this defense may apply.

#### 3.9 Illegality
- The underlying contract involved illegal activity or violated consumer protection laws.

#### 3.10 Release / Waiver
- The creditor previously signed a release or waiver of the debt.

#### 3.11 Failure to Mitigate Damages
- The plaintiff unreasonably allowed interest, fees, or penalties to accumulate when they could have mitigated.

#### 3.12 Laches
- The plaintiff unreasonably delayed filing suit, causing prejudice to you (e.g., lost records, faded memories).

#### 3.13 Improper Venue
- The case was filed in the wrong county or precinct. File a motion to transfer venue within **21 days** of filing your Answer (Justice Court, Rule 502.4).

## 4. Texas Statute of Limitations for Debt

### Limitations Periods by Debt Type

| Debt Type | Limitations Period | Authority |
|---|---|---|
| **Written contracts** (credit cards, personal loans, medical debt) | **4 years** | Tex. Civ. Prac. & Rem. Code § 16.004 |
| **Oral contracts** | **4 years** | Tex. Civ. Prac. & Rem. Code § 16.004 |
| **Promissory notes** | **6 years** | Tex. Civ. Prac. & Rem. Code § 16.004(c) |
| **Debt evidenced by writing** | **4 years** | Tex. Civ. Prac. & Rem. Code § 16.004 |
| **Open accounts (stated)** | **4 years** | Tex. Civ. Prac. & Rem. Code § 16.004 |
| **Judgments** | **10 years** (renewable) | Tex. Civ. Prac. & Rem. Code § 34.001 |
| **Federal student loans** | **No limit** | No federal statute of limitations |

### When the Clock Starts

The statute of limitations begins to run from the date the **cause of action accrues** — generally the date of the last missed payment or the date the debtor breached the agreement.

### Texas Finance Code § 392.307 — The "Zombie Debt" Protection (2019)

This is one of the strongest debtor protections in Texas:
- A **partial payment** on a time-barred debt does **NOT** restart the statute of limitations.
- A **reaffirmation or promise to pay** does **NOT** restart the clock.
- "Any other activity" does **NOT** revive the limitations period.
- The only way to revive a time-barred debt in Texas is through a **new written and signed promise to pay**.
- **Debt buyers are specifically prohibited** from suing after the statute expires, even if payment has been made.
- Debt buyers must provide **written notice** when taking collection actions past the limitations period.

### Federal Protection (CFPB Rule)

12 CFR § 1006.26 — The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau prohibits debt collectors from **suing or threatening to sue** over time-barred debts.

### Important Notes

- The statute of limitations is an **affirmative defense** — you must raise it in your Answer. The court will not raise it for you.
- Even time-barred debts are still legally "owed" — the creditor just cannot enforce them through litigation.
- Collectors **can** still contact you about time-barred debts and request voluntary payment.

## 5. Debt Validation Rights (FDCPA & TDCPA)

### Federal: Fair Debt Collection Practices Act (FDCPA)

**Applies to:** Third-party debt collectors and debt buyers only (NOT original creditors).

#### Debt Validation Notice (15 U.S.C. § 1692g)
Within **5 days** of initial contact, the collector must provide written notice including:
- The amount of the debt
- The name of the creditor to whom the debt is owed
- A statement that the debt will be assumed valid unless disputed within 30 days
- A statement that if disputed, the collector will obtain verification
- A statement that the collector will provide the name and address of the original creditor if different

#### Your Right to Dispute (30-Day Window)
- Within **30 days** of receiving the validation notice, you can dispute the debt **in writing**.
- Upon receiving your dispute, the collector must **cease all collection activity** until verification is provided.
- Send your dispute by **certified mail, return receipt requested**.
- Simply calling the collector is NOT sufficient — it must be in writing.

#### What the Collector Must Provide Upon Dispute
- Verification of the debt (amount, origin, current holder)
- A copy of any judgment (if applicable)
- Name and address of the original creditor (if different from current holder)

### Texas: Texas Debt Collection Act (TDCA / TDCPA)

**Applies to:** Both original creditors AND third-party collectors (broader than FDCPA).

#### Texas Finance Code Chapter 392 — Prohibited Practices

**Harassment/Abuse (§ 392.301):**
- Threatening violence
- Using obscene language
- Making false accusations of criminal conduct
- Threatening arrest or imprisonment

**Unfair Practices (§ 392.303):**
- Collecting unauthorized fees or charges
- Threatening to seize property without legal right
- Threatening to seize homestead or wages (these are exempt under Texas law)

**Deceptive Practices (§ 392.304):**
- Misrepresenting the amount or legal status of debt
- Falsely claiming to be an attorney or government official
- Threatening legal action that cannot legally be taken (e.g., suing on time-barred debt)

**Communication Restrictions:**
- Cannot call outside 9:00 AM - 8:00 PM (without your consent)
- Cannot contact you after learning you have an attorney
- Cannot contact your family, neighbors, or employer about the debt
- You can send a **cease-and-desist letter** (certified mail) demanding they stop all contact

#### Pre-Lawsuit Requirement
Under Texas law, the creditor must send a **written demand letter** at least **30 days before filing** a lawsuit.

## 6. Discovery Process in Debt Cases

### What Is Discovery?

Discovery is the legal process allowing each side to request information and documents from the other side before trial.

### Types of Discovery in Texas

| Discovery Tool | Rule | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| **Requests for Disclosure** | Rule 194 | Mandatory initial exchange of basic case information |
| **Interrogatories** | Rule 197 | Written questions requiring sworn written answers |
| **Requests for Production** | Rule 196 | Demands for documents, records, electronic files |
| **Requests for Admission** | Rule 198 | Requests to admit or deny specific facts |
| **Depositions** | Rule 199-203 | Oral questioning under oath |

### Discovery in Justice Court

Discovery in Justice Court is **limited** — it requires **judge approval** and is restricted to what the judge considers "reasonable and necessary" (Rule 500.9).

### Deadlines

- **Standard response time:** 30 days from receipt of discovery requests.
- **If received by mail:** Add 3 additional days.
- **Justice Court context:** 50 days is sometimes referenced for initial discovery compliance.

### Discovery YOU Should Send to the Plaintiff

As a defendant, you should send the plaintiff discovery to force them to prove their case:

#### Requests for Production — Ask for:
1. The **original signed credit agreement** between you and the original creditor
2. Complete **chain of assignment** documents showing transfer from original creditor to current plaintiff
3. **Account statements** showing all charges, payments, and the final balance
4. **Bill of sale** or portfolio purchase agreement
5. **Business records affidavit** authenticating the documents
6. Proof the plaintiff is **licensed to collect debts** in Texas
7. Any **correspondence** between original creditor and plaintiff regarding your account

#### Interrogatories — Ask:
1. Identify the original creditor and the original account number
2. State the date of the last payment received
3. Describe how the amount claimed was calculated (principal, interest, fees)
4. Identify every entity that owned this debt and the dates of each transfer
5. State whether the plaintiff has the original signed agreement

#### Requests for Admission — Ask the plaintiff to admit or deny:
1. That the plaintiff does not have a copy of the original signed agreement
2. That the plaintiff purchased this account as part of a bulk portfolio
3. That the last payment on this account was more than 4 years ago
4. That the plaintiff is not the original creditor
5. That the total amount includes charges not in the original agreement

### CRITICAL: Responding to Requests for Admission

**WARNING:** If you do not respond to Requests for Admission within the deadline, they are **deemed admitted** — meaning the court treats every statement as true. This can result in **automatic loss** of your case. Always respond on time, even if your answer is "denied."

### Objections to Discovery

You may object to discovery that is:
- Privileged (attorney-client communications)
- Vague or ambiguous
- Overbroad or unduly burdensome
- Seeks irrelevant information
- Requests information equally available to the requesting party

## 7. Pre-Trial Motions

### 7.1 Motion to Compel Discovery

If the plaintiff fails to respond to your discovery requests, file a **Motion to Compel** asking the court to force compliance.
- The court can impose sanctions including fines, attorney's fees, and even case dismissal.
- Conversely, if YOU fail to respond to discovery, the plaintiff can file a motion to compel against you.

### 7.2 Motion for Summary Judgment / Summary Disposition

Either party may file this motion arguing there are no genuine disputes of material fact and they are entitled to judgment as a matter of law.

**In Justice Court (Rule 503.2):** Called "Summary Disposition." The court must grant the motion if:
- No genuinely disputed material facts exist, OR
- The defense lacks evidence of an essential element, OR
- The plaintiff's claim lacks an essential element

**As a defendant, you might file this if:**
- The statute of limitations has clearly expired
- The plaintiff cannot produce any evidence connecting you to the debt
- The plaintiff lacks standing (no proof of assignment)

### 7.3 Motion to Transfer Venue

If the case was filed in the wrong county or precinct, file within **21 days** of filing your Answer (Justice Court).

### 7.4 Motion to Dismiss

If the plaintiff's petition fails to state a valid legal claim, you can move to dismiss.

### 7.5 Motion for Continuance

If you need more time to prepare for trial, file a motion explaining why (e.g., waiting for discovery responses, need to locate witnesses).

## 8. Mediation/Settlement Negotiation

### Pre-Trial Mediation

Many Texas justice courts require pre-trial mediation or a pre-trial conference before setting a case for trial.
- **You must appear.** If the defendant does not appear for pre-trial mediation, the court may enter a **default judgment**.
- The mediator is neutral and cannot provide legal advice.
- Nothing said in mediation can be used against you at trial.

### Settlement Negotiation Strategies

1. **Before trial** is the best time to negotiate — once a judgment is entered, your leverage decreases significantly.
2. **Debt buyers typically purchase debts for pennies on the dollar** (often 2-10 cents per dollar). They may accept far less than the full amount claimed.
3. **Common settlement ranges:** 20-50% of the claimed amount, depending on the strength of their evidence.
4. **Always get the settlement in writing** before paying anything. The agreement should include:
   - The exact amount to be paid
   - That the payment constitutes full and final satisfaction of the debt
   - That the plaintiff will file a dismissal with prejudice
   - That the plaintiff will report the account as "paid/settled" to credit bureaus
   - That the plaintiff releases all claims related to this debt
5. **Do NOT make partial payments** without a written agreement — this could be used as evidence against you.
6. **Lump sum payments** typically get better settlement rates than payment plans.

### Negotiation Tips

- Never admit the debt is yours during negotiations.
- Start your offer low (10-15% of claimed amount).
- If they claim $5,000 and bought the debt for $250, a $1,000 settlement is still profitable for them.
- Use your defenses as leverage: "I plan to raise the statute of limitations defense at trial."
- If the plaintiff cannot produce the original agreement, their case is weak — negotiate accordingly.

## 9. Trial Preparation and What to Expect

### Before Trial

1. **Organize all documents** — your Answer, discovery responses received, evidence, exemption claims.
2. **Prepare an outline** of your key arguments and defenses.
3. **Prepare questions** for cross-examining the plaintiff's witness.
4. **Subpoena witnesses** if needed (you can subpoena the plaintiff's records custodian).
5. **Know the rules of evidence** — hearsay objections are your friend in debt buyer cases. The plaintiff's documents may be inadmissible without a proper business records affidavit.
6. **Dress professionally** and arrive early.
7. **Bring copies** of everything — at least 3 copies (one for you, one for the judge, one for opposing counsel).

### What Happens at Trial

**Justice Court trials are relatively informal** compared to higher courts:

1. **Opening Statements** — The plaintiff goes first, then the defendant.
2. **Plaintiff's Case** — The plaintiff presents evidence and witnesses. The plaintiff bears the **burden of proof** and must prove:
   - A valid agreement/contract existed between the defendant and the original creditor
   - The defendant breached the agreement (stopped paying)
   - The plaintiff owns the debt (chain of assignment)
   - The specific amount owed
3. **Cross-Examination** — You get to question the plaintiff's witnesses. Key questions:
   - "Do you have the original signed agreement?"
   - "Were you present when the defendant allegedly signed this agreement?"
   - "How did you calculate the amount claimed?"
   - "Can you authenticate these documents from personal knowledge?"
4. **Defendant's Case** — Present your evidence and defenses.
5. **Closing Arguments** — Summarize why the plaintiff has not met their burden.
6. **Judgment** — The judge issues a written judgment identifying the winner and any monetary award.

### Key Evidentiary Objections

- **Hearsay:** Most documents from debt buyers are hearsay unless accompanied by a proper business records affidavit from someone with personal knowledge.
- **Lack of Authentication:** The plaintiff must authenticate documents — a debt buyer's employee usually cannot authenticate records created by the original creditor.
- **Best Evidence Rule:** If the plaintiff cannot produce the original agreement, copies may be challenged.
- **Lack of Personal Knowledge:** The plaintiff's witness must have personal knowledge of the facts — reading from a computer screen is not personal knowledge.

### Bench Trial vs. Jury Trial

- **Bench trial** (judge only): Default in Justice Court. Faster, less formal.
- **Jury trial**: Must be requested at least 14 days before trial and requires a $22 fee. Juries may be more sympathetic to individual defendants.

### Recording the Proceeding

There is no state requirement for Justice Court civil hearings to be recorded. If you want a record, you must request it from the court, and you may need to pay for a court reporter.

## 10. Post-Judgment Options

### If You Win

- The case is dismissed, and you owe nothing on that claim.
- If you filed counterclaims and won, the plaintiff may owe you damages.
- Request the court order the plaintiff to pay court costs.

### If You Lose

#### 10.1 Motion for New Trial
- **Justice Court:** Must be filed within **14 days** of the judgment being signed (Rule 505.3).
- **County/District Court:** Must be filed within **30 days** (TRCP Rule 329b).
- Grounds: new evidence discovered, errors of law, verdict against the great weight of the evidence.

#### 10.2 Appeal
- **Justice Court to County Court:** File a bond or Statement of Inability to Afford Costs within **21 days** of judgment. The case is tried completely fresh ("de novo") in county court.
- **County/District Court:** File a Notice of Appeal with the trial court within **30 days** of judgment. The appellate court reviews the record for legal errors.

#### 10.3 Payment Plans
- You can negotiate a payment plan with the judgment creditor.
- Get the agreement in writing with specific terms.
- The judgment creditor is under no obligation to accept a payment plan.

#### 10.4 Claim Exempt Property
- Even after a judgment, you can protect exempt property by filing the appropriate claims (see Section 11).

#### 10.5 Judgment Duration and Renewal
- Texas judgments are enforceable for **10 years**.
- Judgments can be **renewed** before expiration.
- Interest accrues on judgments at the rate specified in the judgment (typically 5% post-judgment).

## 11. Texas Property Exemptions from Collection

Even if a judgment is entered against you, Texas has some of the **strongest debtor protections** in the country.

### 11.1 Homestead Exemption (Tex. Prop. Code § 41.001-41.003)

| Type | Size Limit |
|---|---|
| Urban homestead | Up to **10 acres** |
| Rural homestead (individual) | Up to **100 acres** |
| Rural homestead (family) | Up to **200 acres** |

- Your primary residence **cannot be seized** to satisfy consumer debts.
- **Exceptions:** Mortgage/home equity loan default, unpaid property taxes, mechanic's/materialman's liens.
- Proceeds from the sale of a homestead are exempt for **6 months** after the sale.

### 11.2 Wages / Current Earnings

- **Current wages cannot be garnished** for consumer debts under Texas Property Code Chapter 42.
- **Exceptions:** Child support, spousal maintenance, federal student loans, IRS debts.
- **CRITICAL WARNING:** Once wages are deposited into a bank account, they lose "current wages" protection and may be subject to garnishment. Keep exempt income in a separate account and document its source.

### 11.3 Personal Property (Tex. Prop. Code § 42.001-42.002)

**Aggregate exemption:** Up to **$50,000** (individual) or **$100,000** (family) fair market value.

Exempt items include:
- **Home furnishings** and family heirlooms
- **Food and consumables**
- **Clothing**
- **One motor vehicle** per licensed family member
- **Tools, equipment, and books** used in a trade or profession
- **Jewelry** — up to $12,500 (individual) / $25,000 (family)
- **Two firearms**
- **Athletic and sporting equipment**
- **Pets and domestic animals**
- **Farm/ranch vehicles and implements**
- **Livestock:** 12 cattle, 60 head of other livestock, 120 fowl (with food)
- **Two horses, mules, or donkeys** with saddles/equipment
- **Professionally prescribed health aids**
- **Religious texts**

### 11.4 Retirement Accounts

- **401(k), IRA, Roth IRA, pension plans** — generally exempt while held in the plan.
- **Exception:** May be garnished for court-ordered child support, spousal maintenance, and certain federal debts.
- Monthly distributions from retirement accounts are typically **not** protected once received.

### 11.5 Government Benefits (Fully Exempt)

- Social Security benefits
- SSI / SSDI
- Veterans Administration benefits
- Workers' compensation
- Unemployment benefits
- TANF (Temporary Assistance for Needy Families)
- FEMA disaster benefits
- Child support and alimony received
- Railroad Retirement Board benefits
- Office of Personnel Management retirement

**Bank Account Protection:** If only exempt government benefits are directly deposited into an account, the bank must protect **two months' worth** of deposits. Send an **anti-garnishment letter** to your bank if this applies to you.

### 11.6 Other Exemptions

- **Unpaid commissions:** Up to $12,500 (individual) / $25,000 (family)
- **Education savings accounts** (529 plans)
- **Health savings accounts** (HSAs)
- **Life insurance and annuity benefits**

### 11.7 How to Claim Exemptions

If a judgment creditor attempts to collect against exempt property:
1. File a **Protected Property Claim Form** with the court.
2. File a **motion to dissolve or modify** the writ of garnishment/execution.
3. Provide evidence that the property or income is exempt (bank statements showing direct deposit source, retirement account statements, etc.).

## 12. Default Judgments and Motions to Set Aside

### What Is a Default Judgment?

If you fail to file an Answer by the deadline or fail to appear at trial/mediation, the judge may enter a **default judgment** — granting the plaintiff everything they requested without hearing your side.

### Deadlines to Challenge a Default Judgment

| Court | Deadline | Authority |
|---|---|---|
| **Justice Court** | **14 days** from date judgment was signed | Rule 505.3 |
| **County/District Court** | **30 days** from date judgment was signed | TRCP Rule 329b |

### Extended Deadlines

- If you first learned about the judgment **more than 20 days** after it was signed, your 30-day period begins from the date you received notice or actually learned of the judgment (whichever is first).
- **Maximum outer limit:** 120 days from the date the judgment was signed (90-day discovery window + 30 days to file).
- **Service by publication:** Two-year window to challenge.
- **Active military duty:** Extended time may be available under the Servicemembers Civil Relief Act.

### What You Must Prove

To set aside a default judgment, you must show one of:

**Ground 1: Lack of Proper Notice**
- You were never properly served with the citation/petition.
- You did not receive adequate notice of the hearing.

**Ground 2: Accident or Mistake** (the Craddock test)
You must prove ALL three elements:
1. Your failure to answer/appear was due to **accident or mistake**, not intentional disregard or conscious indifference.
2. You have a **meritorious defense** — a legitimate reason the court should rule in your favor.
3. Setting aside the judgment will **not cause undue delay or harm** to the other party.

### Required Forms

- **Motion to Set Aside Default Judgment and Notice of Hearing** (Form PR-DJ-135)
- **Proposed Order on Motion to Set Aside Default Judgment** (Form PR-DJ-218)

### Filing Procedure

1. The motion must be **sworn** (signed under oath / verified by affidavit).
2. State specific facts supporting your ground for relief.
3. Describe your meritorious defense in detail.
4. Serve the opposing party at least **3 days** before the hearing date.
5. File with the court clerk and request a hearing date.

### If the Motion is Granted

The default judgment is vacated, and the case proceeds as if it were new — you will need to file an Answer and prepare for trial.

## 13. Credit Reporting Disputes

### How Long Negative Items Stay on Your Credit Report

- Most negative items: **7 years** from the date of first delinquency.
- Bankruptcy (Chapter 7): **10 years** from filing date.
- Bankruptcy (Chapter 13): **7 years** from filing date.
- Paid tax liens: **7 years** from date paid.

### Your Rights Under the Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA)

- You have the right to a **free annual credit report** from each of the three bureaus (Equifax, Experian, TransUnion) via AnnualCreditReport.com.
- If denied credit based on your report, you are entitled to a **free copy** and a written explanation.
- You have the right to **dispute** any inaccurate information.

### How to Dispute Credit Report Errors

1. **Obtain your credit reports** from all three bureaus.
2. **Identify errors** — wrong account numbers, incorrect balances, accounts you don't recognize, debts past the 7-year reporting period.
3. **File disputes** online, by mail, or by phone with each bureau:
   - TransUnion: transunion.com/dispute
   - Experian: experian.com/disputes
   - Equifax: equifax.com/personal/disputes
4. **Include documentation** supporting your dispute.
5. The bureau must investigate within **30 days** of receiving your dispute (15 U.S.C. § 1681i).
6. After investigation, the bureau must:
   - Provide results in writing
   - Remove unverified or inaccurate information
   - Exclude disputed information from future reports

### Relevant Texas Laws

- Texas Business & Commerce Code § 20.06 — dispute procedures
- Texas Business & Commerce Code § 20.07 — correction timelines

### Common Violations by Debt Collectors

- **Re-aging** a debt — changing the original delinquency date to keep negative items on your report longer than 7 years. This is illegal under the FCRA.
- Reporting a debt as "new" after selling it to another collector.
- Failing to mark a disputed account as "disputed" with the credit bureaus.
- Continuing to report after receiving a dispute and failing to investigate.

### If the Bureau Fails to Correct Errors

1. File a complaint with the **Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB)** at consumerfinance.gov.
2. Add a **written consumer statement** (up to 100 words) to your credit file explaining the dispute.
3. Contact the **furnisher** (creditor/collector) directly to demand correction.
4. File a **lawsuit** under FCRA — you may be entitled to actual damages, statutory damages ($100-$1,000 per violation), punitive damages, and attorney's fees.

## 14. Counterclaims Under FDCPA/TDCPA

If the debt collector violated the law in attempting to collect from you, you can file **counterclaims** in the same lawsuit.

### FDCPA Counterclaim (15 U.S.C. § 1692k)

**Applies to:** Third-party debt collectors and debt buyers only.

**Available Damages:**
- **Actual damages** — out-of-pocket losses, emotional distress
- **Statutory damages** — up to **$1,000** per case (regardless of actual harm)
- **Attorney's fees** and court costs
- **Class action damages** — up to $500,000 or 1% of the collector's net worth

**Statute of Limitations:** **1 year** from the date of the violation.

**Common FDCPA Violations to Claim:**
- Suing on a time-barred debt
- Failing to send debt validation notice within 5 days
- Continuing collection after receiving a written dispute (without providing verification)
- Misrepresenting the amount owed
- Threatening actions they cannot or will not take
- Calling outside permitted hours
- Contacting you after receiving cease-and-desist letter (except to notify of suit)
- Using abusive or threatening language
- Contacting third parties about your debt

**Strategic Advantage:** Filing an FDCPA counterclaim keeps the case in state court — the plaintiff cannot remove it to federal court when FDCPA is raised as a counterclaim in a pending state case.

### TDCPA / TDCA Counterclaim (Texas Finance Code § 392.403-404)

**Applies to:** Both original creditors AND third-party collectors (broader than FDCPA).

**Available Remedies:**
- **Injunctive relief** — court order to stop the illegal conduct
- **Actual damages** — all losses caused by the violation
- **Deceptive Trade Practices Act (DTPA) remedies** — Texas Finance Code § 392.404 treats TDCA violations as deceptive trade practices, potentially allowing:
  - Up to **3x actual damages** for knowing violations
  - Mental anguish damages
  - Attorney's fees

**Statute of Limitations:** At least **2 years**, arguably **4 years** — significantly longer than the FDCPA's 1-year window.

**Common TDCA Violations to Claim:**
- Threatening to seize homestead or wages (these are exempt in Texas)
- Making false representations about the debt
- Collecting unauthorized charges or fees
- Using threats, coercion, or harassment
- Failing to provide required notices under § 392.307 (debt buyer notice requirements)

### Filing a Counterclaim

1. Include the counterclaim in your **Answer** or file a separate **Counterpetition**.
2. State the specific violations with dates, times, and details.
3. Identify the federal or state statute violated.
4. State the damages you have suffered.
5. Request specific relief (monetary damages, injunction, attorney's fees).

## 15. Common Mistakes Pro Se Defendants Make

### Mistake 1: Ignoring the Lawsuit
The single most common and most damaging mistake. If you do not file an Answer by the deadline, the court enters a **default judgment** — you lose automatically, even if you have strong defenses.

### Mistake 2: Not Responding to Requests for Admission
If you fail to respond to Requests for Admission within 30 days, **every statement is deemed admitted as true**. This can result in immediate judgment against you. Always respond on time.

### Mistake 3: Missing Deadlines
Courts enforce deadlines strictly. Mark every deadline on your calendar. If you need more time, file a motion for continuance **before** the deadline passes.

### Mistake 4: Not Raising Affirmative Defenses in the Answer
Affirmative defenses (statute of limitations, bankruptcy discharge, etc.) must be raised in your Answer. If you forget, you may be permanently barred from using them at trial.

### Mistake 5: Admitting the Debt During Settlement Negotiations
Never say "I owe this money" or "I know I should have paid." These statements can be used against you. Instead: "I dispute the amount and the validity of this claim."

### Mistake 6: Making Partial Payments Without a Written Agreement
A partial payment without a settlement agreement could be used as evidence that you acknowledged the debt — though Texas § 392.307 now prevents this from restarting the statute of limitations.

### Mistake 7: Not Sending Discovery to the Plaintiff
Many pro se defendants play pure defense. Go on offense — send discovery requests forcing the plaintiff to prove they own the debt, have the original agreement, and can verify the amount.

### Mistake 8: Failing to Object to Hearsay Evidence
Debt buyers routinely present documents they purchased in bulk. These documents are **hearsay** unless properly authenticated with a business records affidavit from someone with personal knowledge. If you don't object, the court may admit them.

### Mistake 9: Not Appearing at Pre-Trial Mediation
Even if you filed an Answer, failing to appear at a mandatory pre-trial mediation can result in a default judgment.

### Mistake 10: Talking Too Much in Court
Answer questions directly and briefly. Don't volunteer information. Don't argue with the judge. Don't get emotional. Present your evidence and legal arguments calmly.

### Mistake 11: Not Keeping Records
Keep copies of everything: every document you file, every document you receive, every letter from the collector, proof of mailing, and a log of phone calls with dates, times, and what was said.

### Mistake 12: Assuming the Plaintiff Has Their Evidence Together
Debt buyers often **cannot** produce the original agreement, a complete chain of assignment, or accurate account records. Make them prove every element — many cases are won simply because the plaintiff cannot meet their burden of proof.

### Mistake 13: Not Claiming Exempt Property
Even after a judgment, much of your income and property may be exempt. File the appropriate exemption claims promptly.

## 16. The Plaintiff Side: Filing a Debt Collection Suit in Texas

### 16.1 Court Selection

| Court | Amount | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Justice Court** | Up to **$20,000** | Informal, self-representation common. Exclusive of statutory interest and costs, but inclusive of contractual attorney's fees. |
| **County Court at Law** | **$200.01 - $250,000** (increasing to $325,000 after Sept. 1, 2025) | More formal. Attorney recommended. |
| **District Court** | **Over $250,000** (over $325,000 after Sept. 1, 2025), or any amount with no maximum | Most formal. Attorney strongly recommended. |

### 16.2 Venue — Where to File

Under Rule 502.4(b), a debt case may be filed where:
- The defendant resides
- The contract was to be performed
- The incident giving rise to the claim occurred
- The property is located (for property recovery)

### 16.3 Required Documentation

To successfully prosecute a debt collection case, the plaintiff needs:

1. **Original Credit Agreement / Contract**
   - Signed by the defendant
   - Showing the terms, interest rate, and payment obligations
   - Debt buyers often lack this — a critical weakness

2. **Complete Chain of Assignment**
   - Documents showing every transfer of the debt from original creditor to current plaintiff
   - Bill of sale, assignment agreement, or portfolio purchase agreement for each transfer
   - Each assignment must specifically identify the defendant's account

3. **Account Statements**
   - Showing all charges, payments, fees, and the running balance
   - Demonstrating how the amount claimed was calculated

4. **Charge-Off Statement**
   - The original creditor's final statement showing the charged-off balance

5. **Business Records Affidavit (Tex. R. Evid. 803(6))**
   - Required to authenticate documents as business records (hearsay exception)
   - Must be signed by a qualified witness with personal knowledge of the record-keeping process
   - The affiant must establish that the records were:
     - Made at or near the time of the event
     - Made by or from information transmitted by a person with knowledge
     - Kept in the course of regularly conducted business activity
     - Made as a regular practice of that business
   - **Key issue:** A debt buyer's employee typically cannot authenticate the original creditor's records through personal knowledge — this is a frequent basis for challenging the evidence.

6. **Pre-Suit Demand Letter**
   - Proof that a written demand was sent at least 30 days before filing

### 16.4 Filing the Lawsuit

**Justice Court (Debt Claim):**
- File a **Debt Claim Petition** — the Texas Justice Court Training Center provides forms, and eFileTexas.gov has an interactive interview for "Texas Debt Claim Petition."
- Pay the filing fee (varies by county, typically $50-$100).
- Provide the defendant's name and address for service.

### 16.5 Service Requirements

The plaintiff must have the defendant properly served with the citation and petition.

**Methods of Service (Rule 501.2):**
| Method | Who Serves |
|---|---|
| Personal delivery | Sheriff, constable, or certified process server |
| Certified/registered mail (restricted delivery, return receipt) | Court clerk |
| Court-authorized person (must be 18+) | As approved by court order |

**Alternative Service** (when standard methods fail):
- First-class mail plus leaving copies at defendant's residence with someone 16+ years old
- Any other method the court finds reasonably likely to provide notice

**Service by Publication** (when defendant cannot be found):
- Publication in a newspaper plus posting on the TOPICs website

**Return of Service (Rule 501.3(g)):** The person who serves must file proof of service with the court.

### 16.6 If the Defendant Doesn't Answer — Default Judgment

If the defendant fails to file an Answer by the deadline:
1. The plaintiff requests a **default judgment** from the court (Rule 503.1 in Justice Court).
2. The judge may require the plaintiff to present testimony and supporting documents.
3. If granted, the judgment awards the plaintiff the amount claimed plus court costs and potentially attorney's fees.
4. The defendant has **14 days** (Justice Court) or **30 days** (County/District Court) to file a motion to set aside.

### 16.7 Post-Judgment Collection

After obtaining a judgment, the plaintiff can use:
- **Judgment liens** — filed against defendant's non-exempt real property
- **Writ of execution** — orders the constable/sheriff to seize non-exempt personal property
- **Writ of garnishment** — freezes defendant's bank accounts (subject to exemptions)
- **Turnover order** — court orders defendant to turn over non-exempt assets
- **Post-judgment discovery** — interrogatories, depositions, and production requests to locate assets
